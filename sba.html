What is the SBA Sizing Standard?

The SBA sets the industry standard for small business size. Uses the North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) codes to find various industries. NAICS classifies all economic activity into 20 sectors. NAICS code available here

The SBA set up two widely used scale standards. Most manufacturing and mining industries employ 500 people and many non-manufacturing industries have an average annual revenue of $7.5 million.

500 employees are the most widely used size standard in many common industries such as:

  • Manufacturing
  • Mining
  • hydroelectric power
  • natural gas distribution
  • wholesalers or distributors
  • water transportation
  • publishing (excluding software and internet publishers)
  • physics & life sciences
  • environmental remediation
  • dental practices
  • medical practitioners and etc.

Average annual revenue for services and other non-manufacturing industries is $7.5 million.

  • Agriculture
  • retail trade

  • real estate related activities
  • architectural services
  • professional design services
  • education
  • arts, entertainment and recreation
  • lodging and catering services

Non-manufacturer Rule

Simply put, the Nonmanufacturer Rule allows small businesses to provide products they did not manufacture under the small business closure if SBA offers a waiver.

The non-manufacturer rule is an exception to the requirement that the owner of a small business supplying goods to the government under a closing contract pay at least 50% of his cost of manufacturing the goods. The rule allows small business owners to supply products they do not manufacture as long as the products are from another small business.

Under statutory authority, the SBA may grant a waiver to this rule and allow a large business to perform more than 50 percent of the cost of performing manufacturing contracts if it finds no small business exists in a particular industry.

Qualifying as A Non-Manufacturer

In order to qualify as a non-manufacturer, a small business must have less than 500 employees, be primarily engaged in the retail or wholesaletrade, normally sell the type of item being supplied, and supply the end item of a small business manufacturer, processor, or producer made in the U.S.

In 2011, the SBA updated its regulation to include an additional requirement to be a non-manufacturer:

  • The small business must take ownership or possession of the item(s) with its personnel, equipment, or facilities in a manner consistent with industry practice.

This update to the requirements was intended to eliminate confusion at procuring agencies and prevent practices contrary to the intent of the Small Business Act. More specifically, the change seeks to eliminate situations where the SBA has waived the non-manufacturer rule and the prime contractor essentially subcontracts all services, such as warehousing or delivery, to a large business without taking possession of the products. Additionally, the non-manufacturer rule applies only to procurements that have been assigned a manufacturing or supply North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) code—NAICS Sectors 31-33.

New Online Process for Agents and Certificates

In 2012, paper-based annual returns and certificates were replaced by an automated system developed by the Integrated Acquisition Environment (IAE). The Online Representations and Certifications Application (ORCA), an e-government initiative, has replaced the paper-based Representations and Certifications process. These Representations and Certifications (colloquially known as Reps and Certs) contained contractor information that was stored by the government and made available to purchasing officials and government buyers. By completing this information in advance, you avoid having to submit this information each time a contractor bids on the opportunity. Before ORCA was introduced, contractors had to do just that. I had to submit the same information each time I bid on the opportunity.

ORCA was incorporated into the government’s System for Award Management (SAM) registry database in 2012. Contractors will complete their contact and attestation once they have completed the SAM registration. The government will then verify this information.

The contractor shall include information about the company (including size, company structure, staff, annual turnover, products, services, etc.) in the contact and certification and review this information annually to ensure nothing has changed. verification needed. If contractors don’t update their personnel and certificates when their information changes, the government won’t give them jobs.

Contractors are also responsible for updating their personnel and certifications when the government changes requirements. In 2016, the government changed more than 100 of her requirements and contractors had to check their representatives and certificates to ensure their information complied with those changes.

In addition to the person and certificate, and an active and valid complete SAM registration, the contractor is required to submit her Marketing Partner Identification Number (MPIN) and her DUNS number to the government.

US Federal Contractor Registration (USFCR), a private company, manages customer contacts and certificates. As the government makes changes, the USFCR will review customer information and update it, as necessary. The USFCR also reviews this information annually to coincide with renewal of SAM registrations.
